Physics - Electromagnetism

Chapter 5: Magnetic Effects of Electric Current

Introduction to Electromagnetism

Electromagnetism is a branch of physics involving the study of the electromagnetic force, a type of physical interaction that occurs between electrically charged particles. The electromagnetic force usually exhibits electromagnetic fields such as electric fields, magnetic fields, and light.

Important Concept

When an electric current flows through a conductor, it produces a magnetic field around it. This phenomenon is known as electromagnetism.

Magnetic Field Due to Current Carrying Conductor

The magnetic field produced by a current-carrying conductor can be determined using the right-hand rule. If you grasp the conductor in your right hand with the thumb pointing in the direction of the current, your fingers will curl in the direction of the magnetic field lines.

Important Formula

Magnetic field due to a straight current-carrying conductor: B = (μ₀ * I) / (2 * π * d)

Where:
B = Magnetic field
μ₀ = Permeability of free space
I = Current
d = Distance from the conductor

Key Definition

Electromagnet: A soft metal core made into a magnet by the passage of electric current through a coil surrounding it. The strength of an electromagnet can be easily varied by changing the amount of electric current that flows through it.

Applications of Electromagnetism

Electromagnetism has numerous practical applications in everyday life and industry. Some common applications include electric motors, generators, transformers, magnetic locks, and MRI machines in hospitals.

Practical Example

Electric Bell: The electric bell is a common example of electromagnetism. When the button is pressed, current flows through the electromagnet which pulls the hammer to strike the gong. Simultaneously, the circuit breaks which demagnetizes the electromagnet and the spring brings the hammer back to its original position.

Faraday's Law of Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's law states that whenever there is a change in the magnetic flux linked with a circuit, an electromotive force is induced in the circuit. The magnitude of the induced emf is equal to the rate of change of magnetic flux.

Faraday's Law Formula

Induced EMF: ε = -N * (dΦₑ/dt)

Where:
ε = Induced EMF
N = Number of turns in the coil
dΦₑ/dt = Rate of change of magnetic flux
